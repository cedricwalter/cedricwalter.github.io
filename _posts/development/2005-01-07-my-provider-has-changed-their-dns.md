The **Domain Name System** or **DNS** is a system that stores information about **[host names](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Host_name "Host name")** and **[domain names](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_name "Domain name")** in a kind of [distributed database](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributed_database "Distributed database") on networks, such as the [Internet](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet"). Most importantly, it provides an [IP address](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address "IP address") for each host name, and lists the [mail exchange servers](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mail_exchange_server "Mail exchange server") accepting [e-mail](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-mail "E-mail") for each domain.

The DNS provides a vital service on the Internet, because while computers and network hardware work with [IP addresses](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address "IP address") to perform tasks such as addressing and [routing](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routing "Routing"), humans generally find it easier to work with host names and domain names, for example in [URLs](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator "Uniform Resource Locator") and [e-mail addresses](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-mail_address "E-mail address"). The DNS therefore mediates between the needs and preferences of [wetware](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetware "Wetware") and of [software](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software "Software").
